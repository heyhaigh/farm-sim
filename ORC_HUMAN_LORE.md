# The Two Turnings of One Memory: A History of the Farmers and the Warbands

> Canonical worldbuilding for Ry Farms (fantasy-writer commission, 2026-07-11). The lore is the foundation
> for `ORC_HUMAN_RECONCILIATION_PLAN.md` — the build plan that turns these "design seams" into systems.
> Core conceit: in this world **hatred is a *document*, and documents can be rewritten.**

## I. The Nature of the Two Peoples

To understand the long war between the farming towns and the orc warbands, you must first understand a thing the world itself has largely forgotten: they are made of the same stuff. Every soul in this world — human hand at the plow, orc fist on the axe-haft — is grown from a *memory*. A single remembered document, drawn up out of the great shared record, is read, and from that reading a person condenses: a body with its measures of strength and wit, a temper woven of six threads, a dream they carry unspoken, and the CREEDS they will quote when the ground shakes under them. The memory is the seed. The person is what the seed was told it was.

And here is the quiet, terrible hinge of the whole history. A memory can be read two ways. Read one way — the way the towns read — a memory of *making a thing and giving it away* grows a Designer, a cultivator, a keeper of the ledger of kindnesses. Read the other way — through the inverted lens the warbands read by — that *same memory* grows a Runecarver, a taker, one who carves the mark of ownership into what was another's. The creed **cultivate, and share what you cultivate** becomes, turned over, the creed **raid, and take what others have gathered**. The personality that in a town settles toward collaboration and diligence, in a warband weights toward competitiveness and volatility. Nothing is added. Nothing is invented. The lens is simply reversed, and the same soul stands up telling itself a different story about why it exists.

So the two peoples are not opposites the way fire and water are opposites. They are opposites the way a word and its echo are opposites — the same shape, thrown back changed.

**What the towns are strong at:** they build. They bind. A farmer alone is a modest thing, but farmers braid themselves together — bonds between neighbors, communal projects raised by many hands, a memory written back into the common record so that the dead are not wasted. Their true power is *accumulation across time*: a windmill this generation, a storm-tower the next, a creed handed down clean. They remember in order to *preserve*. They are strong wherever patience compounds. They are blind, often, to threat — a people organized around trust is slow to believe the outstretched hand is a fist. And they are afraid, at the root, of the raid: of the season's whole labor taken in a night, of the ledger of kindnesses torn out and scattered.

**What the warbands are strong at:** they endure, and they take. Where the farmer's power compounds slowly, the orc's power is *sudden* — the concentrated arrival, the season's harvest seized in an hour that took a town a year to grow. Their bonds are warband-bonds and blood-debts: obligations sealed not in shared labor but in shared danger and shared grievance. They remember too — fiercely, exactly — but they remember in order to *avenge*. A warband forgets nothing that was done to it and lets nothing rest unanswered. They are strong wherever endurance and swift concentration win the day. They are blind to the long game, to the compounding worth of a thing left standing and tended. And they are afraid, at the root, of *being forgotten* — of enduring everything and having none of it written down, of a debt going unpaid and therefore unreal.

Each is what the other is not. Each carries, folded up inside its own creed, the seed of the other's. That is the whole tragedy and the whole hope of them.

## II. The Deep History, and the Contested First Blow

No one now living can say truly how contact began, and this is not an accident of lost records — it is the shape of the thing itself.

In the earliest eons the towns and the warbands did not know of one another. Towns are dots on a wide world; they grow, they reach outward with their fences and their roads and their curiosity, and in the fullness of time they *meet* other dots. When two towns meet, a creed can cross between them, a memory can travel, and both are changed by the crossing. This is how the human world knit itself together: meeting, and the trade of remembered things.

The first meeting of a town and a warband is remembered — but it is remembered *twice*, and the two rememberings do not agree.

In the record the towns wrote back, it stands like this: strangers came out of the marchlands, were offered the guest-portion at the fence, and took instead the whole store, and struck the one who offered. *They came to take. We only opened the gate.*

In the record the warbands carved, it stands like this: they came to the settled people worn from the long march, asking the endurance-debt every wanderer is owed, and were turned away at the fence like beasts, and struck first for the insult. *They shut the gate on us. We only answered.*

Both are written down. Both are true to the ones who wrote them. And because in this world *memory is load-bearing and literal* — because the written thing is not a story about what happened but the very substance from which the next generation is grown — there is no court above the two records to say which came first. The truth was perhaps never single. Two peoples, grown from the same substrate, each read the same encounter through its own lens, and each honestly recorded the other as the aggressor. The first raid has no innocent party and no guilty one that memory can produce. It has only two documents that contradict, and children grown from both.

From that doubled wound the cycle turns, and the memory-system is the wheel it turns on. A raid happens. The raided town *remembers* it — not as a mood, but as a written grievance: *Oakhollow remembers the Emberton raid.* That grievance does not fade with the generation that felt it. It is written back into the common record, and from that record the next generation is partly grown — an heir raised from a fresh memory but carrying a creed inherited from a forebear the town remembered. So the child of Oakhollow is born already *knowing* to fear the marchlands, not because the child was ever raided, but because the fear was handed down as a creed, clean and unquestioned, generation upon generation. And the warband's children are born already knowing the settled folk shut the gate. Each retaliation writes a fresh grievance; each fresh grievance grows a fresh generation taught to strike first before it is struck. **The fear is never felt new. It is inherited, and inheritance does not weaken it — inheritance is what preserves it perfectly.**

This is why the war is old past reckoning. Ordinary hatreds burn out; the ones who felt the wrong die, and their grandchildren shrug. But a hatred written into the substrate a people is grown from does not burn out. It is *reissued*, whole, with every generation. The wheel does not slow because nothing is forgotten that was written down.

## III. The Preconception, and Why It Is a Creed and Not a Belief

Ask any farmer's child what an orc is, and the answer comes back smooth and certain: a beast that walks upright, deceitful, less than a person, a thing that takes because taking is all it is. Ask any warband's whelp what the settled folk are, and it is the same shape reversed: soft liars behind their fences, hoarders who would let a wanderer die at the gate, less than people, things that keep because keeping is all they are.

Mark this well: *the child has never met one.* This is the crucial thing. The preconception on both sides is a **creed** — an inherited, unquestioned identity-value, quoted when the ground shakes — and not a **belief**, which in this world means a conviction *earned* by living through something. No living farmer's child fears orcs because an orc wronged *them*. They fear because they were *told*, and the telling came down attached to their very making, so that to doubt it feels like doubting themselves.

And that is exactly why the hatred is at once so unbreakable and so breakable. Unbreakable, because a creed is not reasoned into and cannot be reasoned out of; it is not a conclusion but a foundation. But breakable — because a creed is inherited, not lived, and it has never once been *tested against experience*. It stands only because nothing has yet contradicted it from the far more dangerous direction: the direction of a thing you saw with your own eyes. A creed handed down can be un-handed. A belief you earned yourself cannot be argued away — and *that* cuts both directions.

## IV. The Crack in the Wall

Somewhere in the long middle of the eons, a few on each side began to wonder whether the wheel had to keep turning. Not many. The many are always grown from the creed and content in it. But the memory-substrate is deep and various, and now and again it grows one whose personality runs high in curiosity, or whose source-memory holds — folded in the inverted lens or the upright one — some thread of the peacemaker, the one who counts costs, the one who asks *why*.

The first parleys were disasters, and honesty requires that they be remembered as such. Consider what is actually being asked. A farmer walks unarmed toward a warband to say *let us not do this anymore* — and the warband, grown from generations of the creed *the settled folk are liars whose open hand hides a fist*, reads the overture as exactly the trick their creed told them to expect. Why would it not? The creed is not stupid; it is a compression of a real, doubled history in which deception was in fact used, by both, many times. The one who extends the hand and the one who suspects the hand are each behaving *reasonably given what they were grown from*. And so the early hands were struck off. People died for extending them — died and were written back into the record as *proof the other side cannot be trusted*, so that the very act of seeking peace fed a fresh grievance to the war. This is the cruelest turn of the wheel: for a long age, even the attempt at peace became evidence for the hatred, because a peacemaker betrayed makes the best martyr for the creed.

But not every hand was struck. And here the history narrows to the thing it has been circling all along — because somewhere in the record, and it is a rare and precious passage, there is a first time that two individuals from opposite sides *genuinely trusted one another and were not betrayed.*

The particulars matter less than the mechanism, and the game's own bones tell you the mechanism. A farmer and an orc, thrown together — a spared life, a shared danger, a debt honestly paid where treachery was expected — did a thing neither of their peoples had a word for. They formed a **belief**. Not a creed handed down: a conviction *earned*, on the spot, from a real encounter. *This one did not betray me. I saw it.*

Now understand what that earned belief is, sitting inside a person grown from an inherited creed of hatred. It is a contradiction lodged in the foundation. The creed says *they always betray*; the lived belief says *this one did not, and I was there.* And in this world, an earned belief has a terrible authority a creed does not — because you can point at the passage it came from. You lived it. It cannot be un-told the way a handed-down thing can be un-told. When such a belief collides with the inherited raid-creed, it does not politely coexist. It begins, slowly, to *overwrite*. Not the whole creed at once — one person's earned truth does not unmake an eon of documents. But the crack is real, and it is in the load-bearing wall, and — this is the whole of the hope — it too can be written back into the common record. A belief earned from a real cross-faction encounter can be persisted, and a future generation can be grown partly from *it*, carrying forward not the grievance but the exception, not *they betrayed us at the gate* but *once, one of them did not.*

That is the mechanism of hope, and it is exactly symmetrical to the mechanism of the war. The same system that reissues hatred whole, generation on generation, can reissue a single un-betrayed trust the same way. Memory made the wheel. Memory can carve a different groove for it to turn in.

## V. The Register Beneath the History

What is available to these peoples beyond war? The question is not sentimental; it is structural, and the world answers it precisely.

A soul grown from the same memory as its enemy can, in principle, *recognize itself in that enemy* — because it is not a metaphor here but a fact: the orc across the field is the same source-document you are, read backward. Forgiveness in this world is not a feeling and not a forgetting. Forgiveness is the deliberate **un-writing of an inherited creed** — the choice to let a belief you earned yourself stand against the belief you were handed, and then to write *the new thing* back into the record so it too gets inherited. It is terrifying to do, because a creed is your floor, and to contradict it with your own lived belief is to feel the floor move. It is also the only freedom these peoples have that war does not offer: the freedom to be, in one lifetime, something your making did not dictate.

And nothing here is cheap. One handshake does not end an eon. The un-betrayed trust is a single passage against a library of grievance; the peacemaker is still, on most days, martyred; the creed still grows the many while the belief grows the few. The rift is not erased. But it is no longer *sealed*. In a world where hatred is a document, the discovery that documents can be rewritten is not a small hope. It is the only kind of hope that fits the physics of the place.

## VI. Design Seams — Where This Lives in the Game

These are seeds, not specifications — memory-native hooks for the world to grow into:

- **The orc who was spared.** A raider grown with an inherited creed *avenge the marchlands* who, when a farmer chooses to spare rather than strike him, forms an earned BELIEF — *this settled one did not take what was mine* — and carries a contradiction home into the warband's record. Trace it: the passage is pointable-at.
- **The farmer taught orcs are beasts, and learning otherwise.** The mirror case: a townsfolk grown from the raid-creed who lives one honest encounter and cannot un-see it, their creed and their belief now openly at war in a single sheet.
- **The first-trust moment as a rare, traceable event.** Not common, not scriptable on demand — a precious thing that can happen when the right two personalities (high curiosity, a peacemaker thread in the source-memory) meet under the right pressure, and which, once it happens, is *written back* and can found a later generation.
- **Parley as an alternative to the raid.** When two towns meet, the encounter is a raid *today*. Let it sometimes be a parley instead — an overture that can be honored or read as a trick, with real stakes on the reading, and a crossed creed as the reward for trust that holds.
- **Grievance and reconciliation both persisted.** *Oakhollow remembers the Emberton raid* already rides forward as inherited memory. Let the un-betrayed trust ride forward the same way — so that a future heir can be grown carrying the exception instead of the grudge, and a player can watch, across generations, a creed of hatred slowly overwritten by a belief someone once dared to earn.
